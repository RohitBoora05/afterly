Both feel like missing. Both hurt. But there is a real distinction between grieving the specific person - their presence, their particular way of being, the irreplaceable thing that was them - and grieving the structure of the relationship: the companionship, the routine, the sense of being known, the future that had been planned.

Getting clear on which one you are in changes what you do next.

## How to tell them apart

When you picture being with someone else - not in detail, just in the abstract - does the grief ease slightly? If imagining having a relationship with someone new offers any relief, even small, some of what you are missing is the structure. The companionship, the intimacy, the feeling of partnership - these are transferable things.

If picturing someone else brings no relief - if the loss feels specific to that person in a way that no one else could address - the grief is more person-centred. You are missing them, not just what the relationship provided.

> Neither kind of grief is more valid. But they point in different directions for recovery.

## Why it matters

If you are primarily grieving the structure, some of what you need is to rebuild it - connection, routine, intimacy of the non-romantic kind, a life that has texture and people in it. Sitting in isolation waiting to feel better is the slowest possible path through this kind of grief.

If you are primarily grieving the person, rebuilding the structure will help with some of the pain but will not touch the core of it. The work there is more internal - processing the specific loss, letting the attachment resolve over time, reaching acceptance that this particular person is not part of your future.

## Most grief is both

The two are usually intertwined. You miss them and you miss what the relationship gave you. The question is just where the weight sits - because that tells you where to put your energy.

> Name what you are actually missing. Vague grief is harder to move through than specific grief.
