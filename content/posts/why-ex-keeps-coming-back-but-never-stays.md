They leave. You start to adjust. Then they come back - a message, a conversation, something that reopens everything. You reconnect. Things feel different. Then the same distance creeps back in, and eventually they are gone again. The cycle repeats.

This pattern has a shape, and understanding it is the first step to getting out of it.

## What drives the return

When someone leaves a relationship, they experience their own version of withdrawal. The attachment system does not switch off cleanly. Over time, as the discomfort of absence builds, the brain generates pull toward the familiar. They reach out - not necessarily because they have changed, or because they want a relationship, but because the discomfort of the absence has become acute enough to override whatever made them leave.

The return is driven by their discomfort, not by a genuine shift. This is why things often feel good briefly after reconnection, then return to the same dynamics that ended it before.

> They came back because they missed you. That is real. It is not the same as being ready for what a relationship with you would actually require.

## What the cycle costs

Each time the cycle repeats, the detachment process restarts from close to zero. The neural associations get reactivated. The hope gets renewed. The grief gets deferred rather than processed. Over time, the cycle itself becomes the relationship - a pattern of leaving and returning that substitutes for actual resolution.

People caught in this pattern often describe it as the most painful kind of attachment - more exhausting than a clean break, because it combines grief with hope in a loop that never resolves.

## Breaking the cycle

The cycle continues as long as the return is accepted. No contact - maintained even when they reach out - is what breaks it. Not because it guarantees they will change, but because it removes you from the pattern entirely.

> You cannot heal inside a cycle. You can only heal outside it.
