You know you should block them. You have thought about it. You have maybe hovered over the button. And then you do not do it - or you do it and undo it an hour later. For something so simple, it carries an enormous amount of weight.

Here is why.

## Blocking feels like finality

The attachment system does not experience blocking as a neutral administrative action. It experiences it as closing the door - permanently, definitively, with your own hand. As long as the channel exists, some part of the brain maintains the possibility of contact. Blocking removes that possibility, and the attachment system resists the loss of possibility even when the possibility is causing harm.

This is not irrational. The brain is trying to protect what it valued. Blocking is felt as an act of finality, and finality is painful.

> The resistance to blocking is the attachment system doing its job. It does not mean blocking is the wrong call.

## The hope that blocking threatens

Underneath the resistance is usually hope - some version of "if I block them, they cannot reach me if they change their mind." The channel stays open as a way of keeping the door ajar. What looks like practicality is often hope in disguise.

The question worth asking is: how much is keeping that door open costing you? Every time you check to see if they have reached out. Every time you see their name. Every time the possibility flickers back to life and sets the detachment back.

## What blocking actually does

It removes a source of involuntary inputs. It closes a channel that was keeping the attachment active. It is not a statement about them or about the relationship. It is an act of self-protection that creates the conditions for actual healing - which cannot fully happen while the channel is open and the checking continues.

> Blocking is not giving up on them. It is choosing yourself - specifically, the version of you that exists on the other side of this.
