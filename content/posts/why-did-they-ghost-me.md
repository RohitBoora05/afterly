The question that will not leave you alone: why? What did you do? What changed? Why could they not just say something?

The honest answer is that ghosting is almost never about the person who was ghosted. It is about the person who chose to disappear.

## Conflict avoidance

The most common driver of ghosting is conflict avoidance - a difficulty or unwillingness to tolerate the discomfort of a direct, potentially difficult conversation. For people with high conflict avoidance, disappearing feels easier than the imagined weight of saying something honest. The conversation in their head feels more painful than it would be in reality.

This is not malice. It is emotional avoidance - prioritising their own comfort over your right to a clear ending. The result is harmful regardless of the intent.

> Ghosting says: I was not willing to have a hard conversation. It does not say: you were not worth one.

## Attachment avoidance

People with avoidant attachment styles tend to respond to increasing intimacy or emotional weight by withdrawing. As a relationship develops and emotional stakes rise, the avoidant nervous system can experience that closeness as a threat rather than a comfort. Disappearing - rather than addressing the discomfort directly - is a common avoidant response.

If the ghosting followed a period of increasing closeness, or happened after a moment of vulnerability, avoidant attachment is often a factor.

## The explanation you will not get

Here is the difficult truth: even if you could ask them, the explanation would probably not give you the closure you are looking for. People who ghost are often not fully aware of their own reasons. What they offer, if anything, tends to be vague, deflecting, or unsatisfying.

The closure you need is not in their explanation. It is in accepting that their inability to show up for a hard conversation is the full story - and that it is enough information to work with.

> You do not need their explanation to close this. Their behaviour already told you everything that matters.
