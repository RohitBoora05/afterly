You meet someone new. There's interest, attention, maybe attraction. For a moment - sometimes for days - you feel better. Then the same hollow feeling comes back, and now you're managing two complicated situations instead of one.

This is the rebound pattern. And it's not a character flaw - it's a completely predictable response to a very specific pain.

## What the rebound is actually doing

After a breakup, especially one that touched your sense of worth - did they leave? did they choose someone else? - the brain is in a state of low self-regard. New attention from a new person produces a genuine dopamine response. It feels like relief because neurologically, it is.

But it's temporary relief from a wound it can't close. External validation regulates the feeling in the moment. It doesn't address the underlying state that needs it.

> A new person can distract you from the wound. They can't close it.

## Self-worth is rebuilt from the inside

The research on self-esteem after relationship loss is consistent: lasting recovery of self-worth comes from internal sources - behavioral evidence that you're capable, competent, and okay without external validation. From doing things that matter to you. From showing up for yourself repeatedly in small ways.

New relationships - started before the internal work has happened - tend to carry the same dynamics as the last one, because the same unresolved patterns are doing the driving.

## The timing isn't forever

This isn't an argument against ever dating again. It's an argument for doing the internal work first, so that the next relationship starts from a different place - curiosity and genuine interest, rather than need.

> The best thing you can do for your next relationship is to not need it to fix this one.
