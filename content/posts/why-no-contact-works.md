Most people understand no contact as a strategy - a way to seem less available, or to create space that might bring someone back. That's not what it is. The reason no contact works has nothing to do with your ex and everything to do with your own neurology.

## Every contact reactivates the craving

Romantic attachment runs on the brain's reward system. When you were together, contact - a text, a call, their presence - produced a dopamine response. Your brain learned the association: this person equals reward.

After the breakup, every contact reactivates that association. It doesn't matter if the conversation is painful, neutral, or logistical. The contact itself triggers the reward circuit, tells your brain the attachment is still active, and resets the withdrawal clock.

> No contact isn't cold. It's the only environment where your brain can actually begin to heal.

## Silence lets the baseline reset

When contact stops completely, the brain stops receiving the cue that reinforces the attachment. The reward circuit associated with that person starts to quiet. The dopamine baseline - which was calibrated around them - begins to recalibrate around your actual current life.

This takes time. It doesn't happen in a week. But it cannot happen at all if contact continues, because each instance reactivates the association and restarts the process from close to zero.

## This is why partial contact is worse than none

Occasional contact - a check-in here, a reply there - is neurologically more damaging than consistent contact. Variable reinforcement is the strongest conditioning schedule the brain responds to. The uncertainty of whether contact will happen keeps the craving active at a higher intensity than if contact were predictable.

The brain in "maybe I'll hear from them" mode is more activated than the brain that knows contact is happening. Partial no contact keeps you in that state indefinitely.

> Halfway doesn't count. The brain needs a clean break to start building a new baseline.
