You used to have things you looked forward to. Now they're all still there - the people, the activities, the places - and none of them feel like anything. You go through the motions. The enjoyment isn't there.

This is called anhedonia - the reduced ability to feel pleasure - and it's a documented effect of grief and loss. It's not permanent, and it's not a sign that nothing will ever feel good again.

## What's actually happening

The brain's reward system - which governs motivation and enjoyment - has been running on a specific set of associations for the duration of the relationship. When those associations are removed, the system goes through a recalibration phase. Dopamine responses to other stimuli are temporarily blunted. Things don't feel like they're worth doing because the reward signal is muted.

> Flatness is a phase, not a permanent state. The reward system is recalibrating, not broken.

## You don't wait to want to - you act first

The mistake most people make is waiting until they feel like doing things before doing them. That's backwards. The motivation follows the action, not the other way around. This is called behavioral activation, and it's one of the most well-supported interventions for low mood.

You don't do the thing because you want to. You do the thing, and the wanting gradually comes back. Not immediately. Not always the first time. But the behavior is what primes the reward system to re-engage.

> Don't wait until you want to. Do it once. The wanting comes after.

## Start small

One walk. One meal with someone who makes you feel okay. One thing you used to like, done badly, without expecting it to feel the way it used to. The bar is not enjoyment - the bar is showing up.

The enjoyment returns gradually. You'll notice it in small moments first - a laugh that surprised you, a meal that actually tasted like something, an hour that passed without pain. Those moments accumulate.
