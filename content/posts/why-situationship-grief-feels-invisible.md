You are struggling. Genuinely. And yet when you try to explain it, the response is some version of "but you were not even together." Which does not help. Which actually makes it worse. Because now on top of the grief you have the loneliness of it being invisible.

Situationship grief is real. The fact that it lacks social recognition does not reduce the neurological reality of it.

## Why it does not get acknowledged

The social frameworks we have for relationship loss - the language, the support structures, the understood permission to grieve - are built around defined relationships. Breakups are recognised. Divorce is recognised. "We were kind of seeing each other for eight months but never made it official" does not fit neatly into any recognised category of loss.

This leaves situationship grief in a strange position: real enough to hurt, undefined enough to be dismissed. The person experiencing it often ends up doing the dismissing themselves.

> The absence of a label is not evidence that the loss was small. It is evidence that our frameworks have not caught up with how people actually attach.

## What the invisibility does to recovery

When grief is not acknowledged - by others or by yourself - it does not process properly. It gets minimised and suppressed rather than moved through. Suppressed grief tends to surface sideways: as anxiety, as disproportionate reactions to unrelated things, as a vague low mood that has no obvious source.

Giving the grief its proper weight - saying "this was real and it ended and I am allowed to be sad about it" - is not self-indulgence. It is the first step of actual processing.

## What helps

Treat it like a breakup, because neurologically it is one. No contact if you need it. Space to grieve. Acknowledgement from at least one person who takes it seriously. And stop explaining it to people who will not understand - find the one person who will, and talk to them.

> You do not need everyone to validate this. You need you to validate it. Start there.
