The friendship group was part of the relationship. Now it is part of the aftermath - full of people who know both of you, who might report back, who might have already picked a side, who make every social plan complicated in a way it was not before.

There is no perfect playbook here, but there are principles that tend to make it easier.

## Do not make people choose

The instinct to find out whose side everyone is on, or to make your pain visible enough that people feel obligated to support you, usually backfires. It puts friends in an impossible position and often results in them distancing from both of you to avoid the drama. The friends who will genuinely show up for you will do so without being recruited.

> You do not need every mutual friend. You need a few people who actually show up. Focus there.

## The information problem

Mutual friends are a channel through which information about your ex flows in both directions. You hear things about them. They hear things about you. Every piece of information you receive about your ex is new material for your nervous system to process - and it delays healing. Every piece of information they receive about you gives them a window into your recovery that you may not want them to have.

It is reasonable to ask close friends not to pass along updates in either direction. Most people will respect that if you ask clearly and without drama.

## Social situations where you might both be present

If you know they will be there, decide in advance whether to attend rather than making the decision in the moment. If you go, keep interactions brief and neutral. Do not perform either wellness or suffering. Leave when you need to, without making it a scene.

> You do not owe anyone a show of how you are doing. You owe yourself a way through this that does not make it harder.
