It's been two weeks. Maybe three. You expected to be further along than this. Instead you still think about them every day, still feel the pull, still find yourself checking the phone. Something must be wrong.

Nothing is wrong. Your expectations are just based on a number that doesn't come from science.

## It's not 21 days

The "21 days to break a habit" figure gets repeated everywhere and has almost no empirical basis. The most rigorous study on habit formation - from UCL - found that the average time to form or break a behavioral pattern is 66 days. And that's an average across simple habits. Emotional attachment, which involves multiple overlapping neural systems, takes longer.

> Your timeline is not a measure of how much you loved them. It's a measure of how deeply you were attached.

## Attachment style matters too

People with anxious attachment patterns - those who tend to seek reassurance, fear abandonment, and experience relationships intensely - typically take longer to detach after a breakup. This isn't dysfunction. It's a documented variation in how the attachment system works.

If you've always felt things deeply in relationships, you'll likely feel the ending deeply too. That's consistent, not pathological.

## Progress isn't always visible

Recovery happens in layers. Some of it you can feel - the moments that hurt less, the hours that pass without thinking of them. Some of it is happening below the surface - neural associations weakening, the baseline shifting, your sense of self slowly uncoupling from theirs.

Just because you can't feel progress doesn't mean it isn't happening. The work is accumulating even on the days it doesn't feel like it.

> Every day you stay no contact is a day the old circuitry gets quieter, whether you feel it or not.
