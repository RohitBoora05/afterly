You are still in the thick of it. Then you see something - a photo, a mutual friend mentions something - and it is clear they have moved on. Already. It has been weeks, maybe less. And somehow that is its own separate wound on top of everything else.

Before you let that information mean something, here is what is actually going on.

## They may have started grieving before it ended

When someone ends a relationship, they rarely do it impulsively. Most people who initiate a breakup have been processing the possibility for weeks or months beforehand - withdrawing emotionally, mentally rehearsing the end, beginning to detach while still in the relationship. By the time they leave, they have a significant head start on grief.

What looks like moving on fast is often just the visible end of a process that started long before you knew anything was wrong.

> They did not move on faster than you. They started earlier than you knew.

## What looks like moving on is not always moving on

New activity, new people, apparent cheerfulness - none of these are reliable indicators of emotional state. People manage grief in very different ways. Some withdraw. Some accelerate. Someone throwing themselves into new experiences right after a breakup is often running from the same pain you are sitting with - just in a different direction.

The version of them you are seeing - the highlight reel, the public face - is not the full picture. You are comparing your insides to their outsides.

## Why it hurts more than the breakup itself

Seeing an ex move on triggers the same neural pain response as the original loss - sometimes more acutely, because it introduces new information that the brain interprets as definitive rejection. The hope that something might change gets a hard data point against it.

> Their moving on is not evidence that you did not matter. It is evidence that they are a separate person living their own life. Those are different things.
