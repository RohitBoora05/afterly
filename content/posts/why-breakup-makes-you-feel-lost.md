You try to remember who you were before them and it's hazy. Your interests, your routines, even your sense of what you want - it all feels like it ran through them somehow. Without them, there's a question you don't know how to answer: who am I now?

This feeling has a name and a mechanism. It's not vague or metaphorical.

## Relationships expand the self

Psychologists Arthur and Elaine Aron developed the self-expansion model of relationships: when we fall in love, we incorporate our partner's traits, interests, perspectives, and social world into our own self-concept. Their music becomes music you know. Their friends become your friends. Their way of seeing things becomes part of how you see things.

This is not codependency. It's a normal feature of deep attachment. The self grows to include them.

> You didn't lose yourself. You lost the version of you that included them. That's a real loss - and it's also not permanent.

## The breakup amputates part of who you became

When the relationship ends, the parts of your identity that were built through them go into question. Hobbies you found through them, plans you made around a shared future, parts of your personality that only existed in the context of being with them - all of that needs reprocessing.

This is why breakups feel like more than losing a person. You're also grieving a self.

## What rebuilding looks like

Recovery at this stage isn't just about getting over them - it's about rediscovering who you are when you're not organized around another person. What do you actually want? What do you like when no one's preferences are part of the equation? What does your life look like when it's built around you?

> The question "who am I now?" isn't a sign that you're lost. It's the beginning of actually figuring that out.
