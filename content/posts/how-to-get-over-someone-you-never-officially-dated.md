You are grieving something that was never officially a relationship. Which means the people around you may not fully understand why it hurts this much. Which means you may not fully give yourself permission to grieve it. Which means the grief goes underground and takes longer to move through.

Start here: the label does not determine the loss. The attachment does.

## Why unofficial grief is often harder

Grief after a defined relationship has social support built in. People understand breakups. They ask how you are doing. They give you permission to be sad for a while. Grief after something unofficial has none of that infrastructure. You are expected to be fine relatively quickly, because nothing official ended.

This lack of acknowledgement - from others and often from yourself - means the grief does not get processed properly. It gets pushed down, minimised, told it has no right to be as large as it is. And minimised grief tends to linger longer than acknowledged grief.

> Grief does not require a certificate. It requires an attachment that ended. You had one.

## Give it its real name

The first step is to stop calling it "nothing." What you had was real - real time, real intimacy, real feelings, real hope. The fact that it was not formalised does not make any of those things less true. Name what you are actually grieving: a specific person, a specific connection, a future you had imagined, the hope of something becoming more.

## The no contact question

People often feel unsure whether no contact applies when there was no official relationship. It does. The attachment system does not care about relationship status - it cares about connection and proximity. If you are checking their social media, hoping they will reach out, or finding it hard to function without contact, the same principles apply.

> You do not need an official breakup to justify taking space. You just need an attachment that is making you hurt.
