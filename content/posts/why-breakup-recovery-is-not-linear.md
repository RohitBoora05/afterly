You were doing well. Weeks of actual progress - fewer thoughts, less pain, moments of feeling like yourself again. Then something happens - a trigger, a date, a random Tuesday - and you're back in it. Heavy, raw, like the first week.

It's tempting to read this as failure. It isn't.

## Grief is not a straight line

The oscillation model of grief - developed by researchers Stroebe and Schut - describes recovery as a movement between two states: loss-orientation (feeling the grief, processing it) and restoration-orientation (rebuilding, engaging with life). Healthy recovery involves moving between both, not progressing steadily away from one.

Bad days aren't regression. They're part of the oscillation. The brain is still processing. The fact that you're feeling it again doesn't mean you're back at the start - it means the processing is still happening.

> Feeling it again doesn't mean you're back at the start. It means you're human.

## Triggers don't mean lost progress

A setback triggered by a specific cue - their birthday, a mutual friend's wedding, a song - is different from general deterioration. It's the memory system responding to a specific input, not the whole recovery unraveling. The triggered day is real and it hurts. It's also not undoing the 40 days before it.

## What matters is the trend

If you plot the bad days on a timeline, are they getting further apart? Are the lows less low than they were in week one? Is the recovery from a bad day faster than it used to be? Those are the signals that matter - not whether today felt like progress.

> The trend is the measure. One bad day inside a good trend is still a good trend.
