Most no contact timelines are written to motivate you. This one is written to prepare you - because the gap between what people expect and what actually happens is one of the main reasons people break no contact before it has time to work.

## Week 1: Harder than expected

The first week is acute withdrawal. Your brain has been deprived of its usual inputs - the messages, the presence, the anticipation of contact - and is registering their absence as a threat signal. Physically: disrupted sleep, reduced appetite, chest tension. Mentally: a loop that replays the relationship looking for resolution it will not find.

The urge to reach out peaks around day 3 and then, if you do not act on it, begins to ease. Most people who break no contact do so in the first 7 days - often because day 3 or 4 feels worse than the day of the breakup and they interpret that as a sign something is wrong. It is a sign the process is working.

> Week 1 is not representative of what no contact feels like. It is the worst of it. Everything after is easier.

## Week 2: The long middle

The physical symptoms ease. The mental ones intensify slightly. Rumination peaks - the endless replaying, the hypothetical conversations, the cataloguing of what went wrong. This is the brain processing an unfinished event, and it is normal.

Week 2 is also when the social media pull is strongest. The acute crisis of week 1 has passed, the resolve that carried you through it has not been renewed, and the urge to just check - just once - finds the gap. Build friction now, before you need it.

## Week 3: The first real quiet

Most people experience their first genuine relief somewhere in week 3. Not happiness - just a period, sometimes only an hour, where the constant background noise of the loss quiets. It will come back. But it came quiet once, which means it can again.

Triggers are still active and will be for a while. A song, a place, a date on the calendar - these can pull you back to week 1 intensity without warning. This is not regression. It is the memory system processing the loss in fragments.

> Week 3 is when you get your first evidence that this is actually working. Small evidence. Real evidence.

## Week 4: New baseline forming

By the end of the first month, the neurological work is underway even if the emotional experience has not fully caught up. The reward associations built around that person are starting to weaken. The dopamine system that was calibrated around them is beginning to recalibrate.

The urge to contact them is still present but starts to feel more like a habit than a compulsion. That is a real change. Habits can be managed with structure; compulsions require white-knuckling.

## Weeks 5-8: The shift becomes visible

This is when most people start to feel like themselves again - in flashes at first, then more consistently. The bad days still come. But they are shorter, and the recovery from them is faster. The gap between thinking about them and needing to act on the thought is growing.

UCL research puts meaningful habit change at around 66 days. You are in this window now. The work you put in during the first four weeks is compounding.

## Beyond 60 days

By day 60 and beyond, most people describe the loss as integrated rather than raw. Still present. No longer running everything. The relationship exists in memory without requiring constant processing. You can think about them without needing the thought to resolve differently.

> No contact does not make the relationship disappear. It makes it past tense. That is enough.
