It does not make logical sense. You can list every reason the relationship was wrong for you. You have been through it a hundred times. And yet the pull is still there - sometimes stronger than it was when things were good. The worse the relationship was, the harder it can be to leave behind.

This is not irrationality. It is a specific neurological pattern, and it has a name.

## Intermittent reinforcement

The most addictive reward schedule in behavioural psychology is intermittent reinforcement - when rewards come unpredictably, not consistently. Slot machines use it. So did your relationship, if it cycled between warmth and withdrawal, closeness and distance, good periods and painful ones.

The brain responds to unpredictable rewards by ramping up dopamine production in anticipation. You do not get addicted to something that reliably feels good. You get addicted to something that sometimes feels good - because the uncertainty keeps the reward system in a constant state of seeking.

> The chaos was not incidental. It was the mechanism. The unpredictability is what made the attachment so hard to break.

## Why leaving feels worse than staying

When the relationship ends, the intermittent reward stops entirely. The brain - which has been conditioned to seek the reward through uncertainty - goes into a heightened craving state. This is why people often feel more intensely pulled toward a difficult relationship after it ends than they did when they were in it.

The feelings are not a measure of how right the relationship was. They are a measure of how thoroughly the reward system was conditioned.

## What breaks it

The same thing that breaks any conditioned reward response: complete removal of the cue, sustained over time. No contact removes the variable reward entirely. The brain, no longer receiving unpredictable inputs, gradually stops anticipating them. The craving quiets - not immediately, but reliably.

> You are not addicted to them. You are addicted to the pattern. And patterns can be broken.
