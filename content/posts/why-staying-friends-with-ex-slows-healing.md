You agreed to stay friends. It felt like the right thing - the adult thing, the kind thing. Maybe it was their idea, maybe yours. Either way, you're still in contact, still talking, still in each other's lives in some form.

And something isn't healing.

## Proximity keeps the attachment system active

The brain's attachment system doesn't update based on relationship status. It updates based on behavior and proximity. As long as you're in regular contact - even as "friends" - the neural associations built during the relationship stay active. The dopamine circuitry connected to them keeps firing. The baseline can't reset.

Detachment is a neurological process, not a decision. You can decide to be friends. Your brain doesn't care about the label - it responds to the contact.

> Staying friends right after a breakup isn't closure. It's a slower version of not letting go.

## Friendship after a breakup is usually something else

For most people in the immediate aftermath, "staying friends" functions as hope in disguise - proximity maintained in case things change, or comfort taken from a familiar presence without the vulnerability of admitting you still want more. It's rarely neutral friendship. It's usually one person managing their grief through continued access.

That's not a character flaw. It's just honest about what's actually happening.

## Maybe later - not now

Genuine friendship with an ex is possible. It exists. But it requires that both people have actually detached - that the attachment has resolved, that neither person is using the friendship to manage grief or maintain proximity to a relationship they haven't accepted is over.

That takes time. Months, often. And it can only happen after a real period of no contact, not instead of one.

> If being their friend right now hurts, it's because you're not their friend yet. You're still something else.
