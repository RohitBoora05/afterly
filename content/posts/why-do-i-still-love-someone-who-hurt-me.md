You know what they did. You know how it made you feel. You have gone over it enough times that the case against them is airtight. And yet the feelings are still there - real, persistent, embarrassingly intact. You wonder what is wrong with you.

Nothing is wrong with you. The brain does not work the way we wish it did.

## Love and harm use different neural systems

The experience of loving someone is processed primarily in the brain's reward and attachment systems - the same circuitry involved in bonding, dopamine, and motivation. Harm, betrayal, and pain are processed in different regions - the threat response system, the anterior cingulate, areas associated with danger and self-protection.

These systems operate largely independently. One can be running loud while the other runs loud simultaneously. Knowing someone hurt you does not turn off the attachment system. It just adds a second signal that runs alongside it.

> You still love them and they hurt you. Both are true. The brain has no mechanism for making one cancel out the other.

## Intermittent reinforcement amplifies it

If the relationship involved cycles of warmth and harm - good periods followed by painful ones - the attachment system was conditioned through intermittent reinforcement. This creates a stronger bond than consistent positive experience would. The unpredictability deepens the dependency. The harm becomes entangled with the love in a way that makes them hard to separate.

## What to do with it

You do not have to stop loving them to accept that the relationship was wrong for you. You do not have to hate them to justify leaving. The feelings and the decision can coexist. Recovery does not require you to feel differently - just to act in accordance with what you know, even while the feelings say otherwise.

> You are not foolish for still loving someone who hurt you. You are human. Act on what you know, not just what you feel.
