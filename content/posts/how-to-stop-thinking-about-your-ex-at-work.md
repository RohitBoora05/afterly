You have things to do. Deadlines, meetings, tasks that require actual concentration. And yet your mind keeps pulling back to the same place, mid-sentence, mid-email, mid-conversation. The grief does not take work hours off.

You cannot eliminate the thoughts. But you can manage them well enough to function - and that is what this is about.

## Why work is both harder and easier

Work is harder because concentration requires the prefrontal cortex, which is also doing a significant amount of work managing the emotional load of a breakup. You are running two demanding processes simultaneously, and performance in both suffers.

But work also provides something that pure rest does not: external demand. Tasks with deadlines, conversations that require presence, problems that need solving - these create windows of genuine distraction that feel impossible at home alone. Use them.

> Work is not a distraction from healing. It is one of the few environments that forces your brain to engage with something other than the loss.

## The scheduled worry window

One of the most research-supported techniques for managing intrusive thoughts is the worry window - a specific, pre-scheduled time you set aside to think about the thing. When thoughts arise outside that window, you do not suppress them. You defer them: "I will think about this at 6pm." Then at 6pm, you actually do.

This works because it gives the thought somewhere to go without requiring you to fight it. The brain accepts deferral more readily than suppression.

## When a thought breaks through

Notice it, name it briefly - "that is the grief" - and redirect. Not by forcing the thought out, but by actively engaging with whatever is in front of you. Ask yourself a question about the task. Read a sentence out loud. The redirect needs to be active, not passive.

> You cannot stop the thoughts from coming. You can get better at deciding what to do when they arrive.
