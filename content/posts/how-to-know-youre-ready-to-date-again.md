People will tell you to wait six months. Or a year. Or one month for every two you were together. None of these formulas come from research. The real marker isn't time - it's a specific internal shift that time tends to make possible, but doesn't guarantee.

## The marker is attachment resolution

You're not ready to date when a certain number of days have passed. You're ready when your ex is no longer the measuring stick - when you're not evaluating new people based on how they compare to your ex, not trying to find someone who makes you feel what you felt, and not using new dating as a strategy to prove something or feel better about the breakup.

Attachment resolution means the previous relationship has been processed enough that it's not running in the background of every new interaction.

> You're ready when a new person sounds interesting, not like a solution.

## Green flags from your own nervous system

Curiosity, not desperation. The thought of meeting someone new feels light - interesting, maybe a little exciting - rather than urgent or loaded. You're thinking about what you actually want in a relationship, not just who might want you.

Excitement, not escape. You're dating toward something, not away from something. There's a difference in how that feels in the body - one is expansive, the other is relief-seeking.

## There's no universal timeline

Some people genuinely process a relationship in two months. Others take a year. Both are valid. The variable isn't the length of the relationship - it's the depth of the attachment, the circumstances of the ending, and how honestly the recovery was done.

> Don't date because you're supposed to be ready. Date when the idea of it feels like something you want, not something you need.
